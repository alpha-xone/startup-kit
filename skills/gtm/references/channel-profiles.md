# GTM Channel Profiles

Execution playbook, metrics benchmarks, and known traps for each of the 10 GTM channels. Data confidence tagged at the top of each profile.

---

## 1. Content / SEO

**Data confidence**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ | **Best for**: All models (SaaS, marketplace, e-commerce, services, content)

### How It Works

Create valuable content that ranks for search terms your target customers actually search for. Most commonly: blog posts targeting problem-space keywords, product-led content (comparisons, alternatives, reviews), and data-driven thought leadership.

### Execution Playbook

1. **Find keywords your customers search** — not "broad terms" but "specific pain solutions" (e.g., "referral marketing software for SaaS" not "marketing")
2. **Create content 10x better than what ranks** — longer, more data, real examples
3. **Publish consistently** — 2x/week minimum for the first 6 months
4. **Build backlinks** — guest posts, PR, resource pages, directories
5. **Wait 6-18 months** — SEO compounds. If you run out of patience, you'll miss the curve.

### Key Metrics

| Metric | Healthy | Warning | Red |
|---|---|---|---|
| Monthly organic traffic growth | >20% | 10-20% | <10% |
| Keyword ranking in top 10 | >30 keywords | 10-30 | <10 |
| Post-to-lead conversion rate | >2% | 1-2% | <1% |
| Time to first organic signup | <6 months | 6-12 months | >12 months |

### Success Cases

- **Carrd ($1.5M ARR)**: Pure SEO. Pages rank for every "one-page website builder" variant. No ads. No content marketing beyond product pages. Charged $19/yr.
- **Ahrefs ($100M ARR)**: Data-heavy blog posts. Every article is a sales page. SEO compound effect after 2+ years.
- **Referral Rock ($1.5M ARR)**: Wrote 100+ long-form blog posts targeting specific referral marketing keywords. 18 months before seeing traffic, then compound growth.

### Common Traps

- **"Content marketing will work in 3 months"** — It won't. SEO takes 6-18 months for material impact. If you need cash now, pick a faster channel.
- **Writing for your peers not your customers** — "Thought leadership" pieces don't convert. Problem-solving pieces do.
- **Inconsistent publishing** — 10 posts in 2 weeks then nothing for 3 months = zero compound effect. Consistent > intense.
- **No keyword research** — Writing what you want to write, not what people search for. Every post must be tied to a keyword people actually search.

## 2. Build in Public

**Data confidence**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ | **Best for**: Solo founders, dev tools, creator businesses, any founder who can be transparent

### How It Works

Share your revenue, struggles, decisions, and milestones publicly on Twitter/X, LinkedIn, IndieHackers, or your blog. Transparency builds trust. Trust converts to users, advocates, and customers.

### Execution Playbook

1. **Start a Twitter/X or LinkedIn account** dedicated to your build
2. **Post daily** — what you built, what's working, what broke, a screenshot of your Stripe dashboard
3. **Engage with your community** — reply to every comment, follow other builders
4. **Share failures, not just wins** — the most engaging posts are the honest ones
5. **When you launch, your audience converts** — they've been following the journey for months

### Key Metrics

| Metric | Healthy | Warning |
|---|---|---|
| Followers after 3 months | >500 | <200 |
| Engagement rate | >3% | <1% |
| Conversion from audience to paid users | >2% | <0.5% |
| Revenue attributed to "followers found me" | >10% of revenue | <5% |

### Success Cases

- **Pieter Levels (Nomad List, $1.5M ARR)**: Posts Stripe dashboard monthly. 100K+ Twitter followers. His audience IS his growth channel.
- **Marc Lou (17 products)**: Built and failed in public. Shared every revenue number. 200K+ Twitter followers = distribution for every launch.
- **Jon Yongfook (Bannerbear, $2M ARR)**: Monthly "open startup" metrics. Transparency attracted enterprise clients who wanted to work with someone they trust.

### Common Traps

- **Only posting wins** — "Everything is great!" posts get ignored. "Here's what broke today" gets engagement.
- **Inconsistent** — 3 months of daily posts, then nothing for 2 months. You lose the compounding effect.
- **Selling too early** — "Buy my product!" before you've built trust. No one buys from a stranger.
- **Oversharing sensitive data** — Revenue is fine. User data without permission is not. Competitors can also read what you share.

## 3. Product-Led Growth (PLG)

**Data confidence**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐ | **Best for**: SaaS, tools, platforms

### How It Works

The product itself acquires and retains users. Free tier/trial → activation moment → viral loop (invite team, share output) → organic upgrade to paid. Users self-discover value rather than being sold to.

### Execution Playbook

1. **Free tier or free trial** — make it easy to start with zero friction
2. **Define the "aha moment"** — the specific action after which users convert. Measure it. Optimize for it.
3. **Build a viral loop into the product** — share, invite, collaborate, embed — each of these spreads the product
4. **Conversion funnel** — free user → activated user → paying user → power user → advocate
5. **Monetize at scale** — free users at millions, paid users at 2-5% conversion

### Key Metrics

| Metric | Healthy | Warning |
|---|---|---|
| Free-to-paid conversion | >5% | <2% |
| Time to "aha moment" (first key action) | <24 hours | >7 days |
| Viral coefficient (invites per user × conversion) | >1.0 | <0.5 |
| Net revenue retention | >100% | <90% |
| CAC payback via PLG only | <12 months | >18 months |

### Success Cases

- **Canva ($2.3B ARR)**: Free design tool → share designs → colleagues sign up → some upgrade to Pro. The product IS the growth engine.
- **Slack ($1B+ ARR)**: Free for small teams → team invites more people → hits message limit → upgrades to paid. Viral loop built into the product.
- **Notion ($200M+ ARR)**: Free for individuals → share pages with teams → need more blocks → upgrades. Templates = viral acquisition.
- **Zapier ($140M ARR)**: Free tier (100 tasks/mo) → integrate apps → hit limit → upgrade. Each integration is a distribution channel.

### Common Traps

- **No activation metric** — "Users signed up but didn't stick." Without knowing the activation moment, you can't fix it.
- **Free tier too generous** — Users get everything they need for free forever. Nothing to convert to.
- **Free tier too stingy** — Users can't discover enough value to justify upgrade.
- **PLG before PMF** — Trying to scale a product that people don't love yet. PLG amplifies whatever you have — good or bad.

## 4. Community / Audience

**Data confidence**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐ | **Best for**: Creator businesses, dev tools, B2C, education/products

### How It Works

Build a community (newsletter, Slack/Discord group, podcast, YouTube channel) around the problem space → establish authority → when you launch, the community converts. "Build an audience of people who trust your taste in solutions."

### Execution Playbook

1. **Pick a platform** — newsletter (most owned), podcast (hardest), YouTube (biggest reach), Twitter/LinkedIn (easiest)
2. **Publish consistently** — same cadence, same format, same topic
3. **Solve problems publicly** — answer questions, share frameworks, give away value
4. **Engage directly** — reply to every comment, DM, email. Nurture the relationship before you ask for money
5. **Launch to an audience that trusts you** — launch day should feel like friends buying, not cold sales

### Key Metrics

| Metric | Healthy | Warning |
|---|---|---|
| Email list growth rate | >10%/mo | <5%/mo |
| Email open rate | >40% | <25% |
| Community engagement (posts/day per 100 members) | >5 | <1 |
| Revenue per subscriber per year | >$10 | <$2 |

### Success Cases

- **The Hustle**: Daily business newsletter → 1.5M subscribers → sold to HubSpot for $27M.
- **ConvertKit**: Founder Nathan Barry built a blog audience FIRST → launched to them → earned $8K in 24 hours from a pre-sale.
- **Lenny's Podcast**: Deep product expertise → top 1% podcast → $500K+/year from sponsors + paid community. Niche authority = premium rates.

### Common Traps

- **Trying to sell to an audience you haven't built** — You can't monetize trust you haven't earned.
- **Building on a rented platform** — YouTube algorithm changes → views drop. Email list is the only channel you own.
- **Too broad** — "Business advice" doesn't attract a community. "SaaS pricing for $1-10M ARR companies" does.
- **Audience-first, product-never** — Some founders build an audience and never ship a product. Community = distribution, not a business model.

## 5. Product Hunt Launch

**Data confidence**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐ | **Best for**: B2C SaaS, dev tools, consumer apps, designer tools

### How It Works

Launch your product on Product Hunt. If you hit #1, you get 5,000-50,000 visitors in one day → signups → initial user base → feedback. But: **a Product Hunt launch is not a distribution channel.** It's a one-time event that works for some products and not others.

### Execution Playbook

1. **Build a pre-launch audience** — collect emails on a waitlist before launch. PH traffic converts at 1-5%.
2. **Prepare assets** — GIF demo, compelling tagline, strong first comment with founder story
3. **Coordinate for the right day** — launch Tuesday-Thursday. Avoid holidays and major tech events.
4. **Have a community to vote** — your existing audience voting on launch day is the #1 signal PH ranks by
5. **Follow up** — PH traffic converts poorly. Have a welcome sequence ready to engage the visitors who signed up.

### Key Metrics

| Metric | Good | Great | Exceptional |
|---|---|---|---|
| Upvotes | 200-500 | 500-1500 | 1500+ |
| Visitors on launch day | 5,000-15,000 | 15,000-50,000 | 50,000+ |
| Signup rate from visitors | 5%+ | 10%+ | 20%+ |
| Week-1 retention of PH users | >20% | >30% | >50% |

### Success Cases

- **Buffer ($20M ARR)**: Launched as a landing page with pricing. People clicked "buy" → saw "not ready, leave your email." Validated demand without building anything.
- **Sheet2Site** ($100K ARR): Product Hunt launch was their primary traffic source for months. Founders then moved to SEO.
- **Bannerbear** ($2M ARR): Product Hunt launch → initial traction → built in public to sustain growth after the launch spike faded.

### Common Traps

- **Treating Product Hunt as a distribution strategy** — It's a one-time launch. After 48 hours, the traffic stops. What's your plan for day 3?
- **Not converting traffic** — PH visitors browse. If your product doesn't clearly communicate value in 3 seconds, they're gone.
- **Launching before the product is ready** — First impressions matter. A buggy launch = you may never recover those users.

## 6. Productized Service → SaaS (Stair-Step)

**Data confidence**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐ | **Best for**: Services, agency, consulting founders transitioning to SaaS

### How It Works

Start as a service (freelance, consultancy, agency) → productize what you repeatedly do into a fixed-price service → build software to automate/service it → transition to SaaS. Each step generates cash for the next and teaches you what the market needs (because you've been doing it manually for paying clients).

### Execution Playbook

1. **Start by doing the work manually** for paying clients
2. **Note what you do repeatedly** — that's your product opportunity
3. **Productize the service** — fixed price, fixed scope, packaged deliverable
4. **Build software to automate what you do** — you already know exactly what to build from having done it
5. **Transition clients to the software** — lower cost for them, recurring revenue for you

### Key Metrics

| Metric | Healthy | Warning |
|---|---|---|
| Billable utilization | >70% | <50% |
| Service margin | >40% | <25% |
| Product revenue as % of total | Growing YoY | Flat or declining |
| Client transition rate to product | >20% per year | <10% |

### Success Cases

- **Basecamp (37signals, $100M+ ARR)**: Web design agency → built Basecamp for internal use → started selling it → SaaS grew larger than the agency.
- **DesignJoy ($1M ARR)**: Freelance designer → productized service ($4,995/month unlimited design) → hiring → building software.
- **Rob Walling (Drip, acquired)**: Consulting → built/sold small SaaS (DotNetInvoice) → built Drip → acquired. Each step taught the next.

### Common Traps

- **Won't let go of services** — "But the services make money NOW!" Yes, but they also consume your time. You can't build SaaS while running a full-service agency.
- **Trying to skip the stair-step** — Going straight from "I have an idea" to "venture-scale SaaS" without any market experience is the riskiest path.
- **Services train you to be a service provider** — Clients pay for your time. SaaS customers pay for the product. The sales motion is completely different.

## 7. API / Integrations

**Data confidence**: ⭐⭐⭐ | **Best for**: API-first products, developer tools, platform plays

### How It Works

Build integrations with existing platforms (Zapier, Slack, Google Workspace, Shopify, etc.). Each integration is a distribution channel — users find your product through the platform they already use.

### Execution Playbook

1. **Find where your target users already are** — which tools do they use daily?
2. **Build a deep integration** — not surface-level, solve a real workflow
3. **List in the platform's marketplace/app directory** — Shopify App Store, Zapier integrations page, Slack App Directory
4. **Optimize your listing** — screenshots, clear value prop, use case examples
5. **Measure and double down** — which integrations drive the most signups? Go deeper on those.

### Success Cases

- **Zapier ($140M ARR)**: 5,000+ integrations. Each integration is a distribution channel. Every time Zapier integrates with a new app, users of that app discover Zapier.
- **Shopify apps**: Apps that integrated deeply with Shopify's checkout, inventory, and shipping workflows grew with Shopify's platform growth.

### Common Traps

- **Platform dependency risk** — If the platform builds your feature, you're dead. You need stage 3+ on the Wrapper Depth Scale (proprietary data, vertical knowledge, self-reinforcing flywheel).
- **Integration is not a product** — An integration without a unique value proposition is a feature, not a business.
- **Maintenance burden** — APIs change. Platforms deprecate endpoints. Keep integration quality high or users abandon.

## 8. Cold Email / Outbound (Partial Data)

**Data confidence**: ⚠️ Partial | **Best for**: B2B SaaS, services

### Basic Playbook

1. **Define your ICP (Ideal Customer Profile)** — industry, role, company size, pain point
2. **Source contacts** — LinkedIn Sales Navigator, Apollo, Clay
3. **Write personalized emails** — reference something specific about their company/problem
4. **Follow up** — average deals require 4-7 touches
5. **Track metrics** — open rates, reply rates, meeting booked, closed revenue

### Known Benchmarks (from partial data)

- Open rate: 40-60%
- Reply rate: 3-10%
- Meeting booked: 1-5%
- Source: General B2B email benchmarks, not our primary data

### Notable Traps

- Spray-and-pray — sending the same template to 1,000 people (zero personalization, zero results)
- Too much selling, not enough helping — "We built X" vs "I noticed Y about your company — here's how we solved that for similar teams"
- No follow-up — 80% of sales happen after 5 follow-ups, but most salespeople stop after 1

## 9. Partnership / Affiliate (Partial Data)

**Data confidence**: ⚠️ Partial | **Best for**: SaaS, content businesses

### Basic Playbook

1. **Find complementary products** — tools your target customers already use that serve a different need
2. **Create a partner program** — revenue share (20-30% typical for SaaS), referral links
3. **Recruit partners** — product review sites, complementary tool providers, influencers in your space
4. **Provide partner resources** — assets, tracking, clear terms
5. **Track and optimize** — which partners drive quality leads?

### Known Traps

- Partners not active — signing up 100 partners who never promote = noise. Better to have 5 active partners than 100 passive.
- Cannibalizing direct sales — if you have high organic demand, partners may add cost without incremental revenue.

## 10. Paid Acquisition (Partial Data — Mostly "What to Avoid")

**Data confidence**: ⚠️ Mostly failure data | **Best for**: DTC/consumer at scale (after PMF, never before)

### Why This Is Partial

Our data has few successful "paid acquisition wins" from StarterStory/IndieHackers founders. The data we have is overwhelmingly failure stories: "We spent $X on Facebook ads, got users who never converted, and ran out of money."

### The Only Scenario Where It Works

1. You have proven PMF (organic growth, low churn, strong word of mouth)
2. Your unit economics are proven (LTV > 3×CAC at small scale)
3. You have enough cash to test 3+ months of ad spend without needing it back
4. You're in a high-margin, high-LTV business (SaaS > $50/month, retail > $100 AOV)

### Known Traps

- **"I'll spend $5K on ads and get $10K back"** — In practice, this almost never works. Successful paid acquisition requires months of testing, creative iteration, and landing page optimization.
- **VC-subsidized CAC** — The classic consumer trap. Spend $50 on ads to acquire a customer whose first order is $40. "We'll make it up on LTV" — but they never buy again because they came for the discount, not the brand.
- **iOS privacy changes** — As of 2022, iOS attribution is broken. Facebook ads for apps and DTC are significantly less measurable.
