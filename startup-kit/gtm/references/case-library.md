## Content / SEO

### Carrd ($1.5M ARR)

**Founder**: AJ | **Model**: One-page website builder | **Channel**: Pure SEO

AJ built Carrd as a side project. No ads. No content marketing. No social media. Every page on Carrd ranks for "one-page website builder" and its variants. He charged $19/year for Pro. SEO took 2 years to compound, but once it did, growth became automatic.

**Key move**: Every Carrd page you create is a mini-landing page on the Carrd domain. Hundreds of thousands of Carrd pages indexed = massive SEO footprint with zero content marketing effort.

**Source**: StarterStory

---

### Ahrefs ($100M ARR)

**Founder**: Dmytro Gerasymenko (Vitaliy Khudoshyn CEO) | **Model**: SEO tool | **Channel**: Content marketing

Massive, data-heavy blog posts about SEO. Every article is a sales page. They didn't just write "how to do SEO" — they published original research (industry data, rankings analysis) that other sites link to. Backlinks + useful content = SEO dominance.

**Key move**: Original data research creates backlinks naturally. Don't write "how to optimize for SEO" — publish "we analyzed 1M+ search results and found X, Y, Z." That paper gets cited and linked.

**Source**: Public blog

---

### Referral Rock ($1.5M ARR)

**Founder**: Josh Ho | **Model**: Referral marketing SaaS | **Channel**: SEO

Wrote 100+ long-form blog posts targeting specific referral marketing keywords. "Referral program ideas for SaaS" not "marketing tips." Took 18 months to see significant traffic, but then compound growth kicked in — and the blog became their primary customer acquisition channel.

**Key move**: Hyper-narrow keywords. Target the exact phrases a buyer would search when they're evaluating a referral marketing tool. Not generic, not broad.

**Source**: StarterStory, IndieHackers

## Build in Public

### Pieter Levels (Nomad List, $1.5M ARR)

**Founder**: Pieter Levels (sole founder) | **Model**: Multiple products | **Channel**: Build in Public

Posts his Stripe dashboard monthly. Over 100K Twitter followers. Every product launch goes to an audience that already trusts him. His transparency builds trust, and trust converts.

**Key stats**: Started with $0 MRR. Charged $50 for access to a Google Sheet of city data. Grew via Twitter followers who watched him build.

**Source**: Twitter, IndieHackers interviews

Built (and failed at) 17 products in 12 months. Shared every build, every failure, every revenue number publicly. 200K+ Twitter following. Now every product he launches has immediate distribution.

## Product-Led Growth

### Canva ($2.3B ARR)

**Founders**: Melanie Perkins, Cliff Obrecht, Cameron Adams | **Model**: Design platform | **Channel**: PLG

Free design tool → create a design → share with colleagues → colleagues sign up → some upgrade to Pro. The product IS the growth engine. Every design shared on social media is an ad for Canva.

**Key stat**: Went from 0 to 60M+ monthly users without a sales team.

**Source**: YC, interviews, public data

---

### Slack ($1B+ ARR)

**Founder**: Stewart Butterfield | **Model**: Team communication | **Channel**: PLG

Free for small teams → team invites more people → hits message limit (10K messages free) → upgrades to paid. The viral loop is built into the product: every Slack workspace is a demo for every new member.

**Key insight**: The conversion trigger (message limit) is perfectly timed — you only hit it after you're deeply dependent on Slack.

**Source**: YC, public interviews

---

### Notion ($200M+ ARR)

**Founder**: Ivan Zhao | **Model**: All-in-one workspace | **Channel**: PLG

Free for individuals → share pages with teams → team needs more features → upgrades. Templates shared publicly are a viral acquisition channel (each template download = new Notion user).

**Key insight**: Notion's viral loop is their template marketplace. Templates = use cases = distribution.

**Source**: Public interviews, Notion blog

## Community / Audience

### The Hustle (acquired by HubSpot for $27M)

**Founder**: Sam Parr | **Model**: Daily business newsletter | **Channel**: Community + Email

Built a free daily business newsletter → 1.5M subscribers → sold for $27M. The content was the product AND the distribution. No ads needed.

**Key insight**: Free content → massive audience → multiple monetization paths (ads, sponsors, events, acquisition).

**Source**: Public interviews, StarterStory

---

### Nathan Barry / ConvertKit ($28M ARR)

**Founder**: Nathan Barry | **Model**: Email marketing for creators | **Channel**: Audience-first

Before building ConvertKit, Nathan sold $8K in pre-sales from his existing blog audience. He wrote a book, had a following, and launched to them. His audience WAS the early distribution.

**Key insight**: Build the audience before the product. Selling to 1,000 people who already trust you is easier than pitching 100,000 cold strangers.

**Source**: StarterStory, ConvertKit blog

## Product Hunt Launch

### Buffer ($20M ARR)

**Founders**: Joel Gascoigne, Leo Widrich | **Model**: Social media scheduling | **Channel**: Landing page + Product Hunt

Before any code, Buffer launched a landing page with pricing. If people clicked "buy," they saw "we're not ready — leave your email." Measured click-through rate on PRICING, not "learn more." That validated demand.

**Key insight**: Product Hunt wasn't even a thing yet. The move was: validate pricing before building anything.

**Source**: YC, Buffer blog

## Productized Service → SaaS

### Basecamp (37signals, $100M+ ARR)

**Founders**: Jason Fried, David Heinemeier Hansson | **Model**: Project management | **Channel**: Services → SaaS

Web design agency → built Basecamp for internal use → clients asked to use it → started selling → SaaS grew bigger than agency. The original product was built because they needed it.

**Key insight**: The best SaaS products solve problems their founders have personally experienced.

**Source**: 37signals blog, Rework book

---

### DesignJoy ($1M ARR)

**Founder**: Brett Williams | **Model**: Design → productized service → SaaS | **Channel**: Productized service

Freelance designer → productized service ($4,995/month unlimited design) → hired more designers → building software to manage it. Today: $1M ARR, solo + contractors.

**Key insight**: "I will give you unlimited design for $4,995/month" is a service. "I will give you software to manage design" is a SaaS. The service funds the SaaS.

**Source**: StarterStory, IndieHackers

## API / Integrations

### Zapier ($140M ARR)

**Founders**: Wade Foster, Bryan Helmig, Mike Knoop | **Model**: Automation | **Channel**: API ecosystem

5,000+ app integrations. Each integration = a distribution channel. Every time Zapier integrates with a new app, users of that app discover Zapier. Integrations compound.

## Cold Email / Outbound (Partial Data)
*Note: We have fewer case studies for this channel. The below are from general B2B SaaS knowledge, not our core dataset.*

### General Pattern (from multiple B2B founders)

Founders who succeeded with cold email: defined a tight ICP (not "anyone who might need this"), wrote highly personalized emails (not templates), and followed up 4-7 times. Average reply rate: 3-10%.

**Key insight**: The highest-converting cold emails are not "we built X, want to see?" The hook is: "I noticed [specific thing about your company]. Here's how we helped [similar company] solve it."

## Paid Acquisition (Partial Data — Failure Case Study)

### The "Consumer Trap" (multiple unnamed founders)

"I spent $50 on Facebook ads to acquire a customer whose first order was $40. I told myself we'd make it up on LTV. But the customer came for the discount, not the brand. They never bought again. I was spending $50 to make $40, over and over."

**Key insight**: Paid acquisition only works when:
1. You have proven organic retention (people come back without buying again)
2. Your pricing and margin support the math
3. You've tested at small scale ($500-$1K/test, not $50K)
