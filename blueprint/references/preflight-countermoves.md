# Preflight Countermoves

Every dimension Preflight evaluates (what kills startups) has a proven success countermove (what successful founders did to neutralize that risk). This reference maps each dimension to specific countermoves, real founder examples, and a diagnostic checklist.

---

## Dimension 1: Demand Reality

**Preflight says**: Is there a real pain point? Can't find ≥1 pain point with organic evidence? → KILL.

**Countermove**: Extract commitments, not opinions.

Successful founders don't ask "would you use this?" They create situations where potential users must commit something real — money, time, their professional reputation, or a letter of intent.

**Real examples**:
- **Drew Houston (Dropbox)**: Didn't ask "would you use cloud storage?" He made a prototype video, showed it on Hacker News, and measured signups. 75,000 waitlist signups in 24 hours. Commitment, not opinion.
- **Buffer ($20M ARR)**: Landing page with a "plans and pricing" button. When people clicked "buy," they saw "we're not ready — leave your email." Measured click-through rate on PRICING, not "learn more." That's demand.
- **Mom Test standard**: After 15 conversations, if you don't have ≥3 concrete commitments (pre-order, LOI, signed pilot), the demand isn't proven.

**Diagnostic checklist**:
- [ ] Have you extracted commitments (money, signed LOIs, deposits), not just "that sounds great"?
- [ ] Have you talked to ≥15 potential users using Mom Test technique?
- [ ] Are the people giving you positive feedback also the people who don't need to buy anything from you?
- [ ] What is the cost to the customer of doing nothing? If it's zero, your demand is convenience-level, not pain-level.

## Dimension 2: Competitive Defensibility

**Preflight says**: Competition kills 53% of startups (CB Insights). Do you have a real moat?

**Countermove**: Build one of Helmer's 7 Powers before you scale. Last-mover advantage > first-mover advantage.

Successful founders don't try to out-run competition — they build barriers that make competition irrelevant.

**Real examples**:
- **ConvertKit vs Mailchimp**: Mailchimp had scale, brand, and millions of users. ConvertKit won by Counter-Positioning: Mailchimp couldn't follow creators into specialized email without alienating their general audience. Nathan Barry picked a power Mailchimp couldn't match.
- **Tesla vs GM/Ford**: Counter-Positioning. Legacy automakers couldn't go all-electric without destroying their dealer networks and ICE manufacturing investments. Tesla had no legacy to protect.
- **Walmart vs Amazon**: Amazon built Process Power (logistics, personalization algorithms) and Scale Economies (warehouse network) that Walmart took 20 years to begin matching.

**Diagnostic checklist**:
- [ ] Which of Helmer's 7 Powers does your company have today?
- [ ] Which one will be built in the next 12 months? (You need ≥1, but you don't need all 7.)
- [ ] Could a well-funded competitor copy your product in 3 months? If yes → you have zero powers.
- [ ] What would make it painful for your customers to switch to a competitor? (Integration depth, data portability, workflow dependency, community.)

## Dimension 3: Unit Economics

**Preflight says**: LOOTR Consumer #2 killer (114 cases). CAC > LTV kills.

**Countermove**: LTV:CAC ≥3:1 from day 1. Gross margin >50%. Charge what it's worth, not what competitors charge.

Successful founders obsess over unit economics BEFORE scaling. They validate that each transaction makes money — at small scale — before pouring fuel on the fire.

**Real examples**:
- **Ahrefs ($100M ARR)**: Bootstrapped. Never had the luxury of "grow now, monetize later." Every product decision filtered through "does this improve the unit economics?" Charged $99/mo when competitors charged $29 — because their data was 10x better.
- **Nathan Barry (ConvertKit)**: "If you're not embarrassed by your first pricing, you launched too cheap." Started at $29/mo. Raised to $49/mo when churn didn't budge. Today: $29-$999/mo tiers. LTV:CAC ratio >5:1.

**Diagnostic checklist**:
- [ ] Do you know your LTV and your CAC? If both numbers aren't in a dashboard you look at weekly, you don't.
- [ ] Is LTV:CAC >3:1? If not → either raise prices, reduce churn, or lower acquisition cost before scaling.
- [ ] Gross margin >50%? If not → your business model may not be viable at scale.
- [ ] Does your CAC payback period exceed 12 months? If yes → you're gambling on future funding to survive.

## Dimension 4: Runway & Cash

**Preflight says**: CB Insights #3 (29%). China: 25% survive 6 months, 30%+ can't survive 1 month.

**Countermove**: Default-alive plan. Bootstrapped: profitability at $10K MRR. VC-backed: 24+ months runway.

Successful founders never count on "the next round." They have a plan to reach profitability with the cash they have.

**Real examples**:
- **Ahrefs ($100M ARR)**: Never raised money. Had to be profitable from day 1. Built a cash-generating business first, then invested in growth.
- **Atlassian ($4B+ ARR)**: Bootstrapped for 8 years before raising. Profitable before raising. This discipline forced them to build products people would pay for — not products VCs would fund.
- **YC "Default Alive" standard**: If your current growth rate, sustained, gets you to profitability before you run out of cash, you are "default alive." If it doesn't, you are "default dead."

**Diagnostic checklist**:
- [ ] How many months of runway do you have? <6 = 🔴 emergency. 6-12 = 🟡 tight. >12 = 🟢 healthy.
- [ ] Are you "default alive" or "default dead"? Be honest.
- [ ] What is your monthly burn? Are you tracking it? If burn is increasing faster than revenue → you're default dead.
- [ ] Do you have a kill criterion? ("If we don't hit $X MRR by month Y, we shut down or pivot.") If not → you're flying without instruments.

## Dimension 5: Team Fit

**Preflight says**: CB Insights #4 (20-23%). 85% of AI projects die from "cognitive mismatch."

**Countermove**: Founder-market fit > pedigree. Domain expertise OR obsessive user empathy. Solo founders compensate with speed.

Successful founders either deeply understand the domain they're building in, or they're maniacally obsessed with their users. Both work. Neither is optional.

**Real examples**:
- **Patrick Collison (Stripe)**: Deep domain expertise. Was a developer who hated payment integrations. Built what he wanted to use.
- **Brian Chesky (Airbnb)**: Not a hospitality expert. But lived in Airbnbs for months, talked to hundreds of hosts, became maniacally obsessed with the user experience. Domain expertise can be acquired through obsession.
- **Pieter Levels (Nomad List)**: Solo founder. Compensated for lack of co-founder with speed — shipped 12 products in 12 months. Speed = solo founder's co-founder.

**Diagnostic checklist**:
- [ ] Does anyone on the founding team have deep domain expertise in the industry you're targeting? (Not "I read about it" — "I worked in it.")
- [ ] Has the founder met at least 20 target users in person? If not → start.
- [ ] Solo founder? Have you talked to 50+ users? (Solo founders need 2-3x more user conversations to compensate for the lack of a second brain.)
- [ ] Technical founder in a non-technical industry? Do you have a domain expert advisor or co-founder?

## Dimension 6: Business Model Clarity

**Preflight says**: Overall 10% die from bad business models. "Get traffic first, figure out money later" = 🔴.

**Countermove**: One dollar from one customer before many. One clear revenue stream first.

Successful founders have monetization clarity from day 1. They know exactly who pays, how much, and why.

**Real examples**:
- **Basecamp**: Simple pricing from day 1: $99/month flat. No tiers, no per-seat, no complexity. One plan, one price, one value proposition.
- **Carrd ($1.5M ARR)**: $19/year for Pro. One clear price. 100K+ paying users. Simple business model = simple growth.

**Diagnostic checklist**:
- [ ] Can you explain your business model in one sentence? "We charge [who] $[amount]/[period] for [what value]."
- [ ] Do you have at least one paying customer? If not → everything else is speculation.
- [ ] How many revenue streams? 1 = focus. 2 = 🟡 diversification starting. ≥3 = 🟢 resilient. But get 1 working before adding 2.
- [ ] "We'll monetize later" — are you sure there's a monetization model that fits your product? If you can't articulate it specifically, you're speculating.

## Dimension 7: Product/Tech Feasibility

**Preflight says**: Hardware #1 killer. "Demo ≠ Mass Production."

**Countermove**: Ship ugly. v1 should embarrass you. "If you're not embarrassed by v1, you launched too late."

Successful founders ship before they're ready. The market teaches them what to build. They don't try to build the perfect product in isolation.

**Real examples**:
- **Reid Hoffman (LinkedIn)**: "If you are not embarrassed by the first version of your product, you've launched too late."
- **Pieter Levels**: Nomad List v1 was an ugly Google Sheet with some CSS. Launched it anyway. 2 months of user feedback taught him what to build.
- **Stripe**: v1 was seven lines of code you had to embed. Seven. Lines. Launched it anyway. Developers used it and told them what was missing.

**Diagnostic checklist**:
- [ ] Have you shipped something to a real user in the last 2 weeks? If not → you're building in isolation.
- [ ] Are you "90% done" for more than 2 weeks? The last 10% is the first 90% of what the market needs. Ship at 80% and listen.
- [ ] What's the simplest version of your product that delivers value? Not the version with all the features you planned — the version that does ONE thing well enough that someone would pay.

## Dimension 8: Regulatory & Legal

**Preflight says**: Fatal for Finance (22 cases) and Telecom (25 cases).

**Countermove**: Build compliance as moat, not obstacle. "Only we can do this" because "we understand the rules."

Successful founders in regulated industries don't treat regulation as a barrier — they treat it as a competitive advantage. The regulatory burden keeps out competitors who can't or won't comply.

**Real examples**:
- **Stripe**: Built Atlas (company incorporation) and Issuing (card issuance) by deeply understanding banking regulations. Compliance expertise IS their competitive advantage. Competitors can't easily replicate the regulatory approvals.
- **Coinbase**: Went public in the US by being the most compliant crypto exchange. While competitors got sued by the SEC, Coinbase worked with regulators. Regulatory moat = survival.

**Diagnostic checklist**:
- [ ] If your industry requires a license, do you have a timeline and cost estimate to obtain it? If not → make this priority #1.
- [ ] Do you have a regulatory advisor or lawyer who specializes in your industry? If not → find one.
- [ ] Can you turn compliance into a selling point? "We're the regulated, compliant [X]" — that's a trust signal competitors without compliance can't match.

## Dimension 9: Timing

**Preflight says**: "Too early" kills more than "too late." Webvan was right — 20 years early.

**Countermove**: Catch a wave you didn't create. "Why now?" is the harder question with the bigger payoff.

Successful founders don't create waves — they recognize waves already forming and position themselves to ride them. Every massive startup success was in the right place at the right time for a wave they didn't create.

**Real examples**:
- **Stripe (2010)**: Wave: developer tools were becoming a market. Wave: online commerce was exploding. Wave: "API-first" was becoming the standard. Stripe didn't create any of these — they rode all three.
- **Airbnb (2008)**: Wave: 2008 financial crisis → people needed extra income → willingness to rent spare rooms. Wave: smartphone + mobile photography → good photos of properties. Wave: trust in online transactions was rising.
- **Zoom (2011)**: Wave: video conferencing hardware was improving. Wave: internet bandwidth was increasing. Wave: remote work was slowly growing. Then COVID hit (2020) and Zoom was ready to catch the tsunami.

**Diagnostic checklist**:
- [ ] "Why now?" — can you answer this in one sentence? If your answer is "because we have a great idea," you don't have a timing thesis.
- [ ] What external change (technology, regulation, behavior, economics) makes this the right time?
- [ ] Are you too early? If users need to change their behavior significantly, you're early. How long until behavior catches up?
- [ ] Are you too late? If there are 5+ well-funded competitors already at $10M+ ARR, you may be late. What's your differentiator?

## Dimension 10: Growth & Distribution

**Preflight says**: Neeva ($77.5M couldn't break cold start). Distribution > product.

**Countermove**: One channel, milk it dry, then add a second. Depth > breadth in go-to-market.

Successful founders don't spread their growth efforts across 5 channels. They find the one channel that works and go 10x deeper than anyone else.

**Real examples**:
- **Carrd ($1.5M ARR)**: One channel: SEO. No ads. No content marketing. No social media. Just SEO. Product pages rank for every "one-page website builder" variant.
- **Ahrefs ($100M ARR)**: One channel: content marketing. Data-heavy blog posts about SEO. Every article is a sales page. Took years to compound but now generates millions of organic visits.
- **ConvertKit ($28M ARR)**: One channel at each stage: Early = founder's blog audience. PMF = word of mouth from happy creators. Scaling = content marketing + affiliate program.

**Diagnostic checklist**:
- [ ] What is your ONE primary growth channel? Name it specifically. ("Content marketing" → which platform, which format?)
- [ ] Are you doing at least 80% of your growth effort through this one channel? If not → you're spread too thin.
- [ ] Do you know your customer acquisition cost (CAC) per channel? If you're running multiple channels without knowing per-channel CAC, you're gambling.
- [ ] Where do the first 100 users come from? Name the channel. If you can't → you don't have a distribution plan.

## Dimension 11: Platform Dependency

**Preflight says**: 76% of AI projects die from thin wrappers. But the logic applies to ALL platform-dependent businesses.

**Countermove**: Reach at least Stage 3 on the Wrapper Depth Scale. Proprietary data, vertical knowledge, or community as moat.

Successful founders who build on platforms (API, App Store, Shopify, cloud provider) understand the wrapper depth ladder and refuse to stay at Stage 1 or 2.

**Real examples**:
- **Jasper ($1.5B valuation)**: Stage 3-4. Not just an OpenAI wrapper — built proprietary templates, brand voice training, and a self-reinforcing data flywheel. The more you use Jasper, the better it gets at your brand voice.
- **Shopify apps that survived**: The ones that stayed at Stage 1 (simple feature Shopify could build) died when Shopify built the feature. The ones that reached Stage 3 (proprietary data, deep integrations, merchant community) survived and thrived.
- **Benchmark**: If your platform provider announced they're building your feature next month → would you survive? If the answer is "no," you're at Stage 1 or 2. Move up.

**Wrapper Depth Scale (from Preflight)**:
1. Direct API call → competing on UI/price = 🔴
2. Combine multiple APIs → competing on design and UX = 🟡
3. Embed proprietary data/content/vertical knowledge → competing on domain depth = 🟢
4. Self-reinforcing data flywheel → more users = better product = 🟢
5. Self-built core infrastructure → competing on R&D capability = 🟢

**Diagnostic checklist**:
- [ ] What stage are you on the Wrapper Depth Scale?
- [ ] What % of your margin is consumed by platform/API costs? >30% = 🔴
- [ ] What happens if the platform builds your feature? If you're at Stage 1 or 2, have a plan to reach Stage 3 within 6 months.
- [ ] Do you have proprietary data that competitors — including the platform itself — cannot access?

## Dimension 12: Structural Resilience

**Preflight says**: Is demand driven by hype/narrative/policy, or by independent market need?

**Countermove**: Demand must exist regardless of macro conditions. "Would anyone still need this in a recession?"

Successful founders build products whose demand is anti-fragile — they solve problems that exist in good times AND bad.

**Real examples**:
- **Zoom**: Remote work demand spikes in pandemics AND in economic booms (global teams). Anti-fragile: demand goes up in both extremes.
- **Gusto (payroll)**: Companies need payroll in good times and bad. Maybe more in bad times (need to cut costs, manage cash flow carefully). Essential-need product.
- **QuickBooks (accounting)**: In a recession, businesses need to track expenses MORE carefully, not less. Anti-fragile: demand increases in downturns.

**Diagnostic checklist**:
- [ ] "Would anyone still need this product in a recession?" If the answer is "maybe not" → your demand is economically fragile.
- [ ] Is your growth driven by a temporary hype wave (crypto, AI, remote work boom)? If the hype dies, does demand die?
- [ ] What is the customer's essential need that your product serves? If it's "nice to have" → you're at risk when budgets tighten.
- [ ] "Strip away the buzzwords — what is your business model, really?" Don't let "AI startup" mask "it's actually a consulting business." Don't let "solopreneur" mask "it's freelancing with ChatGPT."
