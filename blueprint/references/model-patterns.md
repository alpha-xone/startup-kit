# Business Model Success Patterns

Different business models require different winning moves. What works for SaaS founders will kill a marketplace. What makes an e-commerce brand thrive will suffocate a services business. This reference catalogs the success patterns specific to each model.

---

## SaaS

### The Winning Pattern

```
Vertical-first → land-and-expand → product-led growth → multi-product
```

1. **Start vertical** — solve one painful workflow for one type of user
2. **Land and expand** — get one team/department using it, then expand within the company
3. **Product-led growth** — free tier/trial → activation → viral → upgrade
4. **Multi-product** — add adjacent products for existing customers

### Critical Metrics

| Metric | Healthy | Warning | Red |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Monthly churn** | <3% | 3-5% | >5% |
| **Annual churn** | <10% | 10-20% | >20% |
| **LTV:CAC ratio** | >3:1 | 2-3:1 | <2:1 |
| **Gross margin** | >80% | 60-80% | <60% |
| **Net revenue retention** | >100% | 90-100% | <90% |
| **CAC payback** | <12 months | 12-18 months | >18 months |
| **NPS** | >50 | 30-50 | <30 |
| **Magic number** | >1.0 | 0.75-1.0 | <0.75 |

### Success Cases

| Company | ARR | Key Pattern |
|---|---|---|
| **ConvertKit** | $28M | Went all-in on "email for creators" when Mailchimp was "email for everyone." Vertical-first strategy: bloggers → course creators → authors → podcasters. |
| **Zapier** | $140M | Land-and-expand via integrations. Each new integration = new user base. 5,000+ integrations = 5,000+ distribution channels. |
| **Ahrefs** | $100M | SEO tool that refused to raise VC. Bootstrapped. Massive content engine (long-form, data-heavy blog posts). Freemium → power users upgrade. |
| **Linear** | $50M+ | "Issue tracking for modern software teams." Vertical-first: not "project management" — "issue tracking for developers who hate Jira." Premium pricing ($8/seat). |

### Known Traps

- **Building horizontal too early** — "We're a platform! Anyone can use it!" → No one will use it because it solves no one's problem deeply enough.
- **Free tier without conversion path** — Free users who never convert are a cost, not an asset.
- **Enterprise too early** — Before $5M ARR, enterprise deals will distract and kill you. They have long sales cycles, custom requirements, and one customer can become >30% of revenue.
- **Feature bloat** — Adding features to reduce churn instead of fixing the core experience. More features = more surface area for bugs, complexity, support.

## Marketplace

### The Winning Pattern

```
Supply constraint → one geo/category → liquidity → expand
```

1. **Constrain supply** — make it exclusive, competitive, gated. Don't let any seller on.
2. **Own one geography or category** — be the best in SF before you expand to LA. Be the best for pet-sitting before you add house cleaning.
3. **Achieve liquidity** — enough supply to satisfy demand, enough demand to keep suppliers earning
4. **Expand surgically** — only after the first geo/category is self-sustaining

### Critical Metrics

| Metric | Healthy | Warning | Red |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Take rate** | >15% | 10-15% | <10% |
| **Liquidity ratio** (fill rate) | >80% | 60-80% | <60% |
| **Supply retention** | >70% annual | 50-70% | <50% |
| **Demand retention** | >60% annual | 40-60% | <40% |
| **GMV per supplier** | Growing | Flat | Declining |
| **Net revenue per transaction** | >0 without subsidies | Break-even | Negative without subsidies |

### Success Cases

| Company | Key Pattern |
|---|---|
| **Airbnb** | Supply-constrained launch: only air mattresses during a design conference in SF. No other city, no other event. Found 3 hosts. Took professional photos → 2-3x booking rate. |
| **Uber** | One city (SF), one vehicle type (black cars), one use case (on-demand luxury transport). Didn't add UberX until SF was self-sustaining. |
| **Etsy** | Handmade-only, vintage-only. Strict curation. Sellers had to apply. Exclusivity = quality = trust. |
| **Substack** | Writer-first. Recruited top writers with advances. Supply-constrained: not everyone could start a Substack with an advance. Quality supply attracted readers. |

### Known Traps

- **Too big too fast** — "We're launching in 10 cities!" → Zero liquidity in all 10. Own one first.
- **Subsidizing both sides** — You pay suppliers AND give discounts to demand. Unsustainable. Subsidize one side at most, and only temporarily.
- **Disintermediation** — After matching, supply and demand go off-platform. If take rate feels too high, they will. Build platform value (insurance, payments, reviews) that makes going off-platform painful.
- **Supply quality spiral** — Opening supply too fast → low-quality suppliers → bad demand experience → demand leaves → good suppliers leave. Constrain supply.

## E-commerce / DTC

### The Winning Pattern

```
Differentiated product → organic content → community + retention → retail expansion
```

1. **Sell something that can't be easily copied** — proprietary design, formulation, or brand
2. **Organic content is your CAC** — Instagram/TikTok/YouTube content that earns attention
3. **Email = retention** — DTC brands live or die by their email list. Repeat purchase rate is everything.
4. **Retail is validation** — getting into Target/Sephora/Whole Foods validates demand AND opens a new channel

### Critical Metrics

| Metric | Healthy | Warning | Red |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Gross margin** | >65% | 50-65% | <50% |
| **Repeat purchase rate** | >35% | 20-35% | <20% |
| **CAC as % of first order** | <30% | 30-50% | >50% |
| **Return rate** | <10% | 10-20% | >20% |
| **Inventory turnover** | >4x/year | 2-4x/year | <2x/year |
| **AOV (average order value)** | Growing | Flat | Declining |

### Success Cases

| Company | Key Pattern |
|---|---|
| **Gymshark** | Instagram influencer strategy before "influencer marketing" existed. Sent free clothes to fitness YouTubers → organic exposure → $500M+ brand. |
| **Warby Parker** | "Try 5 at home" — solved the online glasses purchase risk. Differentiated experience = word of mouth. |
| **Glossier** | Built a blog (Into The Gloss) for 4 years before launching products. 1M+ readers → built-in customer base for launch. Content → community → commerce. |
| **Allbirds** | One product: wool runners. One message: "the world's most comfortable shoe." Simplicity = memorability. |

### Known Traps

- **VC-subsidized CAC** — spending $50 on ads to acquire a customer whose first order is $40. "We'll make it up on LTV" — but they never buy again because they came for the discount, not the brand.
- **Undifferentiated product** — selling the same AliExpress dropshipping product as 500 other "brands." You're competing on ad spend, not brand.
- **Inventory death spiral** — over-ordering inventory → discounting to clear → brand damage → lower margins → can't afford next order. Conservative inventory beats aggressive growth.
- **Single-channel dependency** — all revenue from Facebook/Instagram ads → iOS privacy changes kill attribution → business dies. Build organic channels before it's too late.

## Services / Agency

### The Winning Pattern

```
Specialize deep → productize → scale via process → build SaaS from learnings
```

1. **Go narrow** — "I do X for Y type of company" is worth 3x more than "I do everything for everyone"
2. **Productize** — fixed-price packages, not hourly billing. "Brand identity package: $15K / 4 weeks" not "$150/hour"
3. **Build process** — SOPs for everything: sales, delivery, client communication, handoff
4. **SaaS ladder** — the service teaches you what software to build. Every agency owner is a PM-in-waiting.

### Critical Metrics

| Metric | Healthy | Warning | Red |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Billable utilization** | >70% | 50-70% | <50% |
| **Project margin** | >40% | 25-40% | <25% |
| **Client concentration** | <20% | 20-30% | >30% |
| **Annual client churn** | <20% | 20-30% | >30% |
| **Revenue per employee** | >$150K | $100-150K | <$100K |

### Success Cases

| Company | Key Pattern |
|---|---|
| **DesignJoy** ($1M ARR) | One-person design agency. Productized: "unlimited design for $4,995/mo." No hourly billing. Brett built SOPs, then hired, then built software. |
| **WPCurve** (acquired by GoDaddy) | Unlimited WordPress fixes for $79/mo. Productized a service. Built process. 1,000+ clients → acquisition. |
| **Basecamp** (37signals) | Web design agency → built Basecamp for their own projects → started selling it → SaaS grew bigger than agency. The stair-step in action. |

### Known Traps

- **Founder as bottleneck** — every client needs to talk to YOU. If you're the only one who can deliver, you don't have a business — you have a job.
- **Hourly billing ceiling** — $150/hour × 40 hours/week × 50 weeks = $300K max. You've capped your income at your time. Productize or build a team.
- **Client concentration** — one client >30% of revenue. They leave = you die. Maximum 20% per client.
- **Scope creep** — "Can you also just..." kills margins. Fixed-price packages with clear scope. Change requests = additional cost.

## Content / Media

### The Winning Pattern

```
Niche authority → grow audience → community → monetize (courses, sponsors, products)
```

1. **Pick a niche where you're genuinely authoritative or obsessed**
2. **Publish consistently** — one platform, one format, one schedule
3. **Build an email list** — it's the only channel you own. Algorithms change. Email doesn't.
4. **Monetize from multiple angles** — never rely on one revenue stream (ads, sponsors, courses, products, community memberships)

### Critical Metrics

| Metric | Healthy | Warning | Red |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Revenue per subscriber** | >$2/mo | $1-2/mo | <$1/mo |
| **Email open rate** | >30% | 15-30% | <15% |
| **Revenue diversification** | No source >50% | One source 50-70% | One source >70% |
| **Audience growth rate** | >10%/mo | 5-10%/mo | <5%/mo |

### Success Cases

| Company | Key Pattern |
|---|---|
| **The Hustle** | Daily business newsletter → 1.5M subscribers → sold to HubSpot for $27M. Free content → massive audience → acquisition. |
| **Morning Brew** | Daily business newsletter for millennials → 4M subscribers → $50M+ ARR from ads, events, education. |
| **Lenny's Podcast** | One guy with deep product expertise → top 1% podcast → $500K+/year from sponsors + paid community. Niche authority = premium rates. |
| **Ali Abdaal** | YouTube (productivity) → 5M subscribers → online courses ($5M ARR) + sponsors. Multi-revenue streams: ads, courses, affiliates, books. |

### Known Traps

- **Ad-only monetization** — RPMs fluctuate. Sponsors come and go. Build direct-to-audience revenue (courses, memberships, products).
- **Platform dependency** — YouTube algorithm changes → views drop 50%. Instagram changes = reach collapses. Always own the email list.
- **Content treadmill** — must publish forever to maintain revenue. Build products that generate passive or recurring revenue.
- **Niche too broad** — "Business content" → nobody cares. "B2B SaaS pricing strategy for $1-10M ARR companies" → very specific, very valuable.

## Hardware

### The Winning Pattern

```
Pre-sell → crowd-fund → iterate based on feedback → DTC → retail
```

1. **Pre-sell before manufacturing** — if people won't put money down for a prototype, they won't buy the finished product
2. **Use Kickstarter/Indiegogo** — not for funding (margins are thin), for validation and community building
3. **Iterate with real user feedback** — hardware v1 always has issues. Ship to 100 people and fix before 10,000.
4. **Go DTC first** — own the customer relationship and the margin. Then expand to retail.

### Critical Metrics

| Metric | Healthy | Warning | Red |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Gross margin** | >40% | 25-40% | <25% |
| **Return rate** | <5% | 5-10% | >10% |
| **Inventory turnover** | >4x/year | 2-4x/year | <2x/year |
| **BOM as % of retail** | <25% | 25-35% | >35% |
| **Pre-sale conversion** | >3% | 1-3% | <1% |

### Success Cases

| Company | Key Pattern |
|---|---|
| **Framework Laptop** | Modular, repairable, upgradeable laptops. Pre-sold via email list before manufacturing. Community-driven product development. |
| **Pebble** | Kickstarter record-breaker: $10M raised. Proved demand before manufacturing. (Later acquired by Fitbit.) |
| **Oura Ring** | Niche: sleep tracking. Not "general fitness tracker." High-end positioning ($299+). Built credibility through sleep science research. |
| **Peak Design** | Camera accessories. Every product crowdfunded on Kickstarter first. Built a community of photographers who fund new products before they're made. |

### Known Traps

- **"Demo ≠ mass production"** — A working prototype and a factory-ready product are separated by 6-18 months and $100K-$1M in tooling. Plan for it.
- **Inventory kills** — You ordered 10,000 units, sold 500, now have $200K in unsold inventory eating storage costs.
- **Returns destroy margins** — 15% return rate on a product with 40% gross margin = you're losing money. Packaging, QC, and instructions are as important as the product itself.
- **Retail terms** — 60-90 day payment terms + returns allowed + marketing co-op fees. DTC saves you from this. Go retail only after DTC is profitable.
