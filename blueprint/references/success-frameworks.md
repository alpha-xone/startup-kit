# Seven Classic Success Frameworks

These frameworks are the theoretical backbone of startup success pattern analysis. Each has been battle-tested across decades and thousands of companies. When a founder asks "how do I [build a moat / validate an idea / price my product / find PMF]," map the question to the right framework and walk through the diagnostic.

---

## 1. Helmer's 7 Powers — Hamilton Helmer

**Core question**: "What is your sustainable competitive advantage?"

From *7 Powers: The Foundations of Business Strategy*. A power is a condition that creates the potential for persistent differential returns.

### The Seven Powers

| Power | Definition | Startup Relevance | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Scale Economies** | Unit costs decline with volume | SaaS: infrastructure costs per user drop with scale. Hardware: BOM costs drop | Amazon Web Services |
| **Network Effects** | Value increases with users | Marketplace, social platforms, communication tools | Airbnb, Slack, WhatsApp |
| **Counter-Positioning** | Incumbent can't copy without destroying existing business | Disruptive business models the incumbent can't match | Netflix vs Blockbuster, Tesla vs GM |
| **Switching Costs** | Pain of leaving > gain of switching | Enterprise SaaS, deeply integrated tools | Salesforce, SAP, AWS |
| **Brand** | Customers pay premium for perceived value | Luxury, trust-based, status goods | Apple, Hermès, Stripe |
| **Cornered Resource** | Exclusive access to valuable asset | Patents, exclusive partnerships, talent moats | Pfizer patents, exclusive distribution deals |
| **Process Power** | Proprietary operational capability | Company-specific processes that can't be copied | Toyota Production System, Netflix personalization |

### Startup Diagnostic

When evaluating a startup's defensibility:
1. Which of the 7 powers does it have today?
2. Which can it realistically build in 12 months?
3. Zero powers = zero defensibility. At least one is non-negotiable for venture scale.
4. Bootstrappers can survive without powers at small scale (<$1M ARR) if they have strong unit economics and low competition.

> **Key Helmer insight**: "Power must be created before it is needed. If you wait until you feel competitive heat, it's too late."

## 2. Thiel's 7 Questions — Peter Thiel

**Core question**: "Are you building a monopoly or just competing?"

From *Zero to One*. Every great business answers these seven questions before scaling.

### The Seven Questions

| Question | What It Tests | Red Flag Answer |
|---|---|---|
| **The Engineering Question**: Can you create breakthrough technology instead of incremental improvements? | Is your tech 10x better, not 10%? | "It's like X but with a better UX" |
| **The Timing Question**: Is now the right time to start this? | Are you too early or too late? | "We're in the [current hype] space" |
| **The Monopoly Question**: Are you starting with a big share of a small market? | Small pond → big fish strategic sequence | "The market is $500B — we just need 1%" |
| **The People Question**: Do you have the right team? | Founder-market fit, co-founder trust | Co-founders who just met, remote-only from day 1 |
| **The Distribution Question**: Do you have a way to actually deliver your product? | Go-to-market strategy, not just product | "It will go viral" or "We'll do SEO" without specifics |
| **The Durability Question**: Will your market position be defensible 10-20 years from now? | Moat durability, not just first-mover | "We'll move fast and figure it out" |
| **The Secret Question**: Have you identified a unique opportunity that others don't see? | Contrarian but correct insight | "Everyone agrees this is a great idea" |

### Startup Diagnostic

Score each question 0-2:
- 0 = Can't answer / don't know
- 1 = Have an answer but unproven
- 2 = Answer is validated with evidence

A startup that scores <7 is in trouble. Below 5 = likely to fail.

> **Key Thiel insight**: "Brilliant invention is not enough. You need the whole package: technology, timing, monopoly, team, distribution, durability, and a secret."

## 3. "Do Things That Don't Scale" — Paul Graham

**Core question**: "What unscalable move will create your first 100 true users — and become your moat?"

From the classic 2013 YC essay. The most counterintuitive startup truth: founders who manually recruit and serve their first users create an advantage that competitors can't copy.

### The Unscalable Moves

| Move | What It Looks Like | Famous Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Manual recruitment** | Physically find and onboard early users one at a time | Stripe founders installed their payment API for first 100 companies themselves |
| **Concierge service** | You personally do what your product will eventually automate | Airbnb founders took professional photos of every host's apartment |
| **Founder-salesforce** | You are the sales team — you close every deal, learn every objection | Collison brothers closed Stripe's first enterprise deals in person |
| **Fragile, narrow product** | The product only works for one use case with one type of user — and that's the point | Facebook only for Harvard. Uber only for SF black cars. |
| **Over-attention to quality** | Obsess over details that don't scale but create delight | Steve Jobs personally checked every pixel of the original iPhone UI |
| **Fake-it automation** | Manually do behind-the-scenes what appears automated | Early Zapier: humans manually moved data while the "automation" UI appeared to handle it |

### Startup Diagnostic

For a pre-launch or early-stage startup:
1. Who are your first 10 users? Name them.
2. What will you personally do for them that your product cannot do yet?
3. What do you learn from doing it manually that shapes v2?
4. If you can't name 10 people and describe a manual move, you're not ready to scale.

> **Key Graham insight**: "The question to ask about an early stage startup is not 'is this company taking over the world?' but 'how big could this get if the founders did things that don't scale?'"

## 4. The Mom Test — Rob Fitzpatrick

**Core question**: "Are you learning from customer conversations or just collecting false positives?"

From the book *The Mom Test*. The single most important framework for demand validation. The name comes from the insight that your mom will lie to you about your idea being good — and so will everyone else if you ask the wrong questions.

### The Rules

| Rule | What It Means | Bad Question | Good Question |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Talk about their life, not your idea** | Understand their actual behavior, not their opinion of your solution | "Would you use an app that does X?" | "How do you currently solve X? Walk me through the last time." |
| **Ask about specifics in the past, not generics about the future** | Past behavior predicts, future promises don't | "Would you pay $50/mo for this?" | "What's the most you've paid for a tool to solve this?" |
| **Listen for the commitment, not the compliment** | Compliments are useless. Commitments (pre-orders, LOIs, deposits) matter. | (After a demo) "This is really cool!" | "Can we set you up right now? Here's the price." |
| **Avoid pitching** | If you're talking more than they are, you're selling your idea to yourself | 10-minute product pitch | 90/10 silence — they talk, you listen |
| **Look for the "scary truth"** | If they use workarounds, hacked-together solutions, spreadsheets → real pain. If they don't → not real pain. | (No question — assumption) | "What happens if you don't solve this problem? What's the cost of inaction?" |

### The Meeting Flow

1. **Intro** (2 min): "Thanks for meeting. I'm working on [problem space]. I'd love to understand how you currently handle [problem]." — NOT pitching
2. **Their story** (15+ min): Questions about their current workflow, past attempts, pain points
3. **Commitment check** (at the end, optional): "Based on what we discussed, I could build something that [specific value]. If I come back with a working version in 2 weeks, would you pay for it right then?"

### Startup Diagnostic

After 10-15 Mom Test conversations:
- How many revealed a "scary" problem (not just an annoyance)?
- How many described a workaround or manual process they hate?
- How many committed to something concrete (pre-order, pilot, LOI)?
- Less than 3 concrete commitments from 15 conversations = the idea might not be real.

> **Key Fitzpatrick insight**: "You aren't allowed to tell them what their problem is. They aren't allowed to tell you what to build. Both are forms of lying."

## 5. Lean MVP Loop — Eric Ries

**Core question**: "Are you building the smallest thing that teaches you something?"

From *The Lean Startup*. The Build-Measure-Learn feedback loop. Not "build a crappy product" — "build the minimal experiment."

### The Loop

```
IDEAS → BUILD (MVP) → MEASURE (data) → LEARN (insight) → (pivot or persevere) → IDEAS ...
```

### MVP Types (ranked from cheapest to most expensive)

| MVP Type | What It Is | When To Use | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Explainer video** | 2-minute video showing the product concept | Before any code, test if the concept resonates | Dropbox's original explainer video generated 75K waitlist signups |
| **Landing page + waitlist** | One page: problem, solution, CTA to sign up | Test if people care enough to give an email | Buffer: landing page before any code → 120 signups → started building |
| **Concierge / Wizard of Oz** | You manually provide the service behind the scenes | Complex service-based products | Zappos: founder bought shoes at retail, shipped them → proved demand before inventory |
| **Single-feature product** | One feature that solves the core pain | Products with a clear single value prop | Twitter started as just status updates. Instagram started as just filters + photos. |
| **Piecemeal / Frankenstein** | Stitch together existing tools (no-code, APIs) instead of building | Platform/automation ideas | Early Zapier: manually connected APIs before building the full platform |

### Startup Diagnostic

Before building anything:
1. What is the ONE hypothesis you're testing? (Not three. One.)
2. What's the cheapest MVP that can test it?
3. What is the success metric? (Not "people like it" — a number)
4. What will you do if the hypothesis is disproven?

> **Key Ries insight**: "The MVP is not the smallest product. It is the smallest experiment. Sometimes the smallest experiment is a conversation, not code."

## 6. Hormozi's Value Equation — Alex Hormozi

**Core question**: "Why would anyone pay for this — and how much?"

From *$100M Offers*. A mathematical framework for pricing and positioning that separates $100K products from $1M+ products.

### The Equation

```
Value = (Dream Outcome × Perceived Likelihood of Achievement) / (Time Delay × Effort & Sacrifice)
```

| Variable | What It Means | How To Increase Value |
|---|---|---|
| **Dream Outcome** ↑ | What the customer actually wants (not what you sell) | Make the outcome more desirable, more specific, more vivid |
| **Perceived Likelihood** ↑ | How much the customer believes YOU can deliver it | Social proof, case studies, guarantees, specificity, authority |
| **Time Delay** ↓ | How long until they get the result | Faster onboarding, instant wins, "Quick Start" version |
| **Effort & Sacrifice** ↓ | How hard the customer has to work | Done-for-you > done-with-you > DIY. Automation, templates, service |

### Pricing by Pain

The equation maps directly to pricing power:

| Pain Level | Value | Pricing Power | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Existential** (lose job, go bankrupt, die) | ∞ | Charge anything | Life-saving drug, bankruptcy attorney |
| **Major financial** ($10K-$100K impact) | Very high | 10-30% of value saved | Tax optimization, hiring tool, CRM |
| **Productivity** (hours saved) | Moderate | Per-seat or per-hour pricing | Project management, automation tools |
| **Convenience** (nice to have) | Low | $10-50/mo | To-do list app, basic design tool |

### Startup Diagnostic

For pricing:
1. What is the customer's Dream Outcome? (Not "use our software" — what does using it get them?)
2. On a scale of "convenience" to "existential," where does your product land?
3. If they don't buy your product, what is the cost? (Money, time, risk, missed opportunity)
4. Does your pricing reflect that cost? If it saves $10K, do you charge $3K or $10/mo?

> **Key Hormozi insight**: "Make an offer so good, people feel stupid saying no."

## 7. Rachleff's PMF Test — Andy Rachleff

**Core question**: "Are users pulling the product out of your hands?"

From Benchmark Capital co-founder Andy Rachleff. The simplest, most brutal PMF test there is.

### The Test

**"You have product-market fit when you are overwhelmed with usage — usually to the point where you can't even make major changes to the product because so many people are depending on it."**

### Proxy Signals (ranked from strongest to weakest)

| Signal | Strength | What It Looks Like |
|---|---|---|
| **Organic growth without marketing** | 🔥 Strongest | Users signing up through word of mouth, not paid channels |
| **Users would be "very disappointed" without you** | 🔥 Strong | Sean Ellis test: "If [product] disappeared tomorrow, how would you feel?" ≥40% say "very disappointed" |
| **Revenue growth is constrained by supply, not demand** | 🟡 Good | You can't hire/build fast enough to meet demand |
| **Retention curve flattens** | 🟡 Good | After some period, churn approaches zero — users don't leave |
| **Customers are buying faster than you can onboard** | 🟡 Good | Onboarding queue is your bottleneck |
| **High NPS** | 🟢 Useful but weak | NPS >50 alone doesn't mean PMF — it can be politeness |
| **Growing revenue** | 🟢 Useful but weak | Growing revenue from paid ads ≠ PMF |

### Startup Diagnostic

- **Pre-PMF**: Your job is to find PMF. Speed of learning > quality of product. Talk to users. Run experiments. Pivot fast.
- **Post-PMF**: Your job is to scale. Quality of execution > speed of learning. Don't break what's working.

> **Key Rachleff insight**: "A startup's job is to find PMF before running out of money. Everything else is noise."

## Framework Selection Guide

| Founder's Question | Right Framework | Quick Diagnostic |
|---|---|---|
| "How do I build a moat?" | Helmer's 7 Powers | How many of the 7 powers can you claim today? (Need ≥1) |
| "Is my idea good enough?" | Thiel's 7 Questions | Score each question 0-2. Below 7 = trouble. |
| "How do I get my first users?" | Graham's Unscalable Moves | Can you name 10 people and describe a manual move? |
| "How do I validate this idea?" | The Mom Test | After 15 conversations, how many concrete commitments? |
| "How big should v1 be?" | Lean MVP Loop | What's the ONE hypothesis you're testing? |
| "How much should I charge?" | Hormozi's Value Equation | Where on the convenience→existential scale is your product? |
| "Do I have PMF yet?" | Rachleff's PMF Test | Are you overwhelmed with usage? If you have to ask, probably not. |
