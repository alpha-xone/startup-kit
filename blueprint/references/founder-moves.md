# Founder Move Library

Repeatable, tested "plays" that successful founders run. These are not strategies — they are specific, copyable actions. Each move includes: what it looks like, when to use it, data backing it, real examples, and a checklist for executing it.

---

## Move 1: Charge from Day 1

**What it looks like**: Before you have a polished product, charge for access. Pre-sell. Offer consulting. Take waitlist deposits. The moment someone can use your product — even a bare MVP — there is a price tag.

**When to use**: Validation stage ($0 → $1K MRR). Any business model.

**Data backing**: 80% of StarterStory founders making $100K+/year charged from day 1 or pre-sold before they had a working product. The ones who waited "until it was ready" typically waited forever.

**Real examples**:
- **Nathan Barry (ConvertKit, $28M ARR)**: Pre-sold an email course for $8K in 24 hours before writing a single lesson.
- **Pieter Levels (Nomad List, $1.5M ARR)**: Charged $50/person for access to a barely-functional spreadsheet of city data. Revenue > no revenue.
- **Buffer ($20M ARR)**: Launched a landing page with pricing before any code existed. If people clicked "buy," they saw "we're not ready yet — leave your email." Conversion rate validated demand.

**Execution checklist**:
- [ ] Can a stranger give you money today for your product/service? If not → add a way.
- [ ] Is your pricing visible on your landing page? If not → add it.
- [ ] Do you have a "buy now" button that works? If not → build it before any other feature.
- [ ] Have you pre-sold to at least 3 people? If not → find 3 people with the problem and offer them a pre-launch deal.

**Contraindications**: Don't charge if your product is genuinely pre-alpha (core functionality doesn't work and using it would create a negative experience). But "pre-alpha" should last <2 weeks.

## Move 2: Build in Public

**What it looks like**: Share your revenue, struggles, decisions, and milestones transparently on Twitter/X, LinkedIn, IndieHackers, or your blog. Post screenshots of your Stripe dashboard. Share what worked and what failed.

**When to use**: All stages. Most effective at Validation and PMF stages.

**Data backing**: Build-in-public founders grow their Twitter following 5-10x faster than those who don't. They see 2-3x higher conversion from audience to paid users. This is because transparency builds trust, and trust converts. StarterStory + IndieHackers analysis shows that "transparent sharing" is the #2 growth tactic (after word of mouth) for solo founders at <$500K ARR.

**Real examples**:
- **Pieter Levels (Nomad List)**: Posts his Stripe dashboard monthly. Over 100K Twitter followers from his transparency alone. His audience IS his growth channel.
- **Marc Lou (multiple products)**: Built 17 products in 12 months, shared every build, every failure, every revenue number. His 200K+ Twitter following is his distribution for every new launch.
- **Jon Yongfook (Bannerbear, $2M ARR)**: Publishes monthly "open startup" metrics: revenue, churn, expenses. His transparency attracted enterprise clients who wanted to work with someone they trusted.

**Execution checklist**:
- [ ] Do you have a Twitter/LinkedIn account where you post about your product? Start posting once/day.
- [ ] Have you shared a revenue screenshot? Do it. It's terrifying. It works.
- [ ] Do you share failures, not just wins? Followers trust you more when you show the struggle.
- [ ] Is your "build in public" regular? At least 3x/week. Consistency > perfection.

**Contraindications**: Don't build in public if you're in a highly competitive stealth market where every feature advantage matters. Don't share user data without permission.

## Move 3: Micro-Niche Domination

**What it looks like**: Be the absolute best for a tiny, specific audience. "CRM for dog groomers with 2+ locations" not "CRM for small businesses." "Email marketing for professional bloggers making $10K+/mo" not "email marketing."

**When to use**: PMF and Scaling stages. Works for SaaS, services, content.

**Data backing**: Micro-niche SaaS companies have 2x higher win rates and 40% lower churn than generalist SaaS. This pattern is so consistent that it's a law: narrower target = higher conversion = faster PMF.

**Real examples**:
- **ConvertKit ($28M ARR)**: "Email marketing for professional creators" — when every competitor was "email marketing for everyone." Narrow positioning = clear messaging = higher conversion.
- **FYI ($200K ARR)**: "Document collaboration for creative teams" — not "document tool for businesses." Narrow = less competition.
- **Referral Rock ($1.5M ARR)**: "Referral marketing software for SaaS companies" — not "marketing tool." Every blog post targets SaaS referral marketing keywords.

**Execution checklist**:
- [ ] Can you describe your target customer in ONE sentence that includes: who they are + what they do + what makes them specific?
- [ ] Would a stranger read your homepage and immediately know if it's for them or not? If there's any ambiguity → your positioning isn't narrow enough.
- [ ] Have you ever said "no" to a customer because they don't fit your niche? If not → you're not narrow enough.
- [ ] Is your niche growing? (Don't pick "dog groomers in Tulsa" — pick "dog grooming businesses in the US." Specific but scalable.)

**Contraindications**: Don't niche down so far that your TAM is <$10M. Dog groomers in Tulsa = too small. Dog grooming businesses in the US = good. Don't niche yourself into a corner with no room to expand.

## Move 4: The Stair-Step Approach (Rob Walling)

**What it looks like**: Build small → sell for cash → build bigger → sell for more → build real company. Start with services/consulting → productize what you repeatedly do → launch SaaS → scale.

**When to use**: Bootstrapped founders with limited capital. Works for SaaS, services.

**Data backing**: TinySeed founders who stair-stepped had 70% higher survival rate than those who went straight to a venture-scale startup. The pattern reduces risk at each step by generating cash before committing to the next level.

**Real examples**:
- **Rob Walling (Drip, acquired)**: Ran a consulting business → built and sold small SaaS products (DotNetInvoice, HitTail) → built Drip → acquired by Leadpages. Each step taught him something and generated cash for the next.
- **37signals (Basecamp, $100M+ ARR)**: Web design agency → built Basecamp for internal use → started selling it → SaaS grew larger than the agency. The classic stair-step.
- **Brett (DesignJoy, $1M ARR)**: Freelance designer → productized service ($4,995/mo "unlimited design") → hiring designers → building software to manage it.

**Execution checklist**:
- [ ] Are you currently doing any client/consulting work? If not, consider it as a cash generation + market learning step.
- [ ] What do you repeatedly do for clients? That's your product opportunity. Productize it.
- [ ] Do you have at least one cash-generating asset while you build your SaaS? If not → you're betting 100% on the SaaS working. Stair-stepping = you bet on multiple things with increasing stakes.
- [ ] What would you sell TODAY if someone offered you $50K for your side project? Think about who would buy it.

**Contraindications**: Not for VC-funded startups where speed-to-scale matters more than risk reduction. Not for founders who hate client work.

## Move 5: Product-Led Growth (PLG) Flywheel

**What it looks like**: Free tier/trial → activation moment ("aha!" point) → viral loop → organic upgrade → paid plan. The product itself acquires and retains users.

**When to use**: Scaling stage and beyond. SaaS and tools primarily.

**Data backing**: PLG companies have 30-50% lower CAC than sales-led peers. Their net revenue retention is 10-20% higher because users self-discover value rather than being sold to.

**Real examples**:
- **Canva ($2.3B ARR)**: Free design tool → users create designs → share designs with colleagues → colleagues sign up → some upgrade to Pro. The product is the growth engine.
- **Slack ($1B+ ARR)**: Free for small teams → team invites more people → hits message limit → upgrades to paid. The viral loop is built into the product: every Slack workspace is a demo for every new member.
- **Notion ($200M+ ARR)**: Free for individuals → share pages with teams → team needs more blocks → upgrades. Templates shared publicly are a viral acquisition channel.

**Execution checklist**:
- [ ] Do you have a free tier or free trial? If not → add one (unless your average deal size is >$10K/year).
- [ ] What is your product's "aha moment"? The specific action after which users convert. If you don't know it → you can't design a PLG funnel.
- [ ] Is there a natural "share" step in your product? (Invite team member, publish template, export design) → if not, can you add one?
- [ ] What's your free-to-paid conversion rate? <2% = product isn't delivering enough value in the free tier. >5% = healthy.

**Contraindications**: PLG doesn't work for high-touch enterprise sales ($50K+ ACV) where the buyer isn't the user. Doesn't work for products where the free experience can't show meaningful value.

## Move 6: Do Unscalable Things (Paul Graham)

**What it looks like**: Manually onboard every early user. Hand-write welcome emails. Call customers on the phone. Fix bugs live during their session. Do things that would be impossible at 10,000 users — because you have 10, not 10,000.

**When to use**: Validation stage. $0 → $1K MRR.

**Data backing**: Nearly every iconic startup did unscalable things early on. This is not a nice-to-have — it's how you learn what to build and earn the first users' loyalty before you have a product good enough to stand on its own.

**Real examples**:
- **Airbnb**: Founders took professional photos of every host's apartment in NYC. Driving around, ringing doorbells. Unscalable. Increased bookings 2-3x.
- **Stripe**: Collison brothers personally installed Stripe for their first 100 customers. "Hey, we'll come set it up for you." Unscalable. Taught them every pain point in the integration process.
- **Zapier**: Manually connected APIs behind the scenes while their "automation" UI made it look like it was happening automatically. Unscalable. Validated demand and taught them which integrations to build first.

**Execution checklist**:
- [ ] For your next 5 new users, personally onboard them. Don't send them to a docs page. Get on a call. Watch them use your product.
- [ ] What is the most painful onboarding step? Can you personally do it for the user? Do it.
- [ ] Send a personal email (not automated) to every new signup. Ask: "What made you sign up? What problem are you trying to solve?"
- [ ] After 10 personal onboardings: what pattern did you see? That's your next feature. Not your roadmap — what you observed.

**Contraindications**: Don't keep doing unscalable things once you have PMF. The point is to learn what to build and earn early loyalty, then automate/scalable-ize what you learned.

## Move 7: The Empty-Room Demo

**What it looks like**: Before you've built anything, show a barely-working prototype (or even a Figma mockup) to target users. Watch silently where they hesitate, what confuses them, what they click first. Don't explain. Don't pitch. Just watch.

**When to use**: Idea stage to Validation. Before any significant development.

**Data backing**: 60% of features in successful products were added AFTER the first paying customers started using the product. This means the features you think are important before you have users are probably wrong. Empty-room demo catches this early.

**Real examples**:
- **Dropbox**: Drew a Figma prototype of a "magic folder that syncs." Showed it to 10 people. Watched where they paused versus where they nodded. Built v1 based on those observations.
- **Every successful YC company**: YC's motto is "talk to users." But founders who SHOW instead of TELL get 3x more useful feedback. The Demo Day pitch is a refinement of hundreds of empty-room demos.

**Execution checklist**:
- [ ] Create a prototype (Figma, paper sketches, barely-working code) that a user can interact with.
- [ ] Find 5 target users. Not friends. People with the actual problem.
- [ ] Give them the prototype. Say: "Try to [accomplish the core task]." Say nothing else.
- [ ] Watch. Note every hesitation, every confused look, every "what does this do?" That's what you need to fix.
- [ ] Don't explain. If you have to explain, the prototype is wrong.

**Contraindications**: Don't do this with polished products — users will focus on polish feedback, not core usability. Use rough prototypes.

## Move 8: Pain-Ladder Pricing

**What it looks like**: Price based on the size of the problem you solve, not the cost of building the product. If you save a company $10K/month → charge $3K/month. If you make someone's life 5% more convenient → charge $10/month. Map your pricing to THEIR pain, not YOUR effort.

**When to use**: PMF and Scaling stages. When you have enough usage data to understand the actual value delivered.

**Data backing**: The difference between $100K ARR SaaS companies and $1M+ ARR SaaS companies is almost always pricing strategy, not product quality. The $1M+ companies charge based on value delivered, not based on "what competitors charge."

**Real examples**:
- **Linear ($50M+ ARR)**: $8/seat/month. Jira is free or $7.75/user for Standard. Linear charges MORE because their users HATE Jira and Linear solves that pain. They're not competing on price — they're pricing the pain of using Jira.
- **Ahrefs ($100M ARR)**: $99-$999/mo depending on usage. SEO is worth thousands per month to their users. They price accordingly.
- **Superhuman ($100M+ ARR)**: $30/month for EMAIL. Gmail is free. Charge 10x for 10x better product. Premium positioning attracts premium users who churn less.

**Execution checklist**:
- [ ] What is the cost to your customer of NOT using your product? (Money, time, risk, missed opportunity.) Quantify it.
- [ ] Take that number. Charge 10-30% of it. That's your price.
- [ ] Have you raised your prices in the last 6 months? If not → raise them 20% and measure churn. If churn doesn't spike, raise again.
- [ ] What do competitors charge? Ignore it. They don't solve the same pain in the same way. Your pricing is about YOUR value, not THEIR price.
- [ ] Do you have a "call us" enterprise tier? If you're B2B and don't have an enterprise tier, you're leaving 2-5x revenue per large customer on the table.

**Contraindications**: Pain-ladder pricing only works when you genuinely deliver high ROI. If your product is commoditized and the pain isn't acute, competing on price might be the only strategy. But first, ask: can you make the pain acute by targeting a narrower audience?
